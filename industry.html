Industry 4.0

Industry 4.0, also known as the Fourth Industrial Revolution, refers to the integration of advanced technologies such as the Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), big data analytics, robotics, and cyber-physical systems into manufacturing and industrial processes. This transformation aims to create smart factories where machines are interconnected, able to communicate, and make autonomous decisions to optimize production (Kovaitė and Stankevičienė 2019).

Key Features of Industry 4.0

  • IoT: Connecting machines and devices to collect and exchange data.
  • Artificial intelligence (A) and Machine Learning (ML): Enhancing decision-making processes and predictive maintenance.
  • Big Data Analytics: Analyzing vast amounts of data to improve operational efficiency.
  • Cyber-Physical Systems: Integrating physical processes with digital technologies.

Risk linked to Industry 4.0

Industry 4.0 introduces new types of risks and amplifies existing ones, including

  • Cybersecurity Risks: Increased connectivity exposes systems to cyberattacks, data breaches, and malware. Protecting sensitive data and ensuring the security of interconnected devices becomes critical.
  • Operational Risks: Dependence on automated and interconnected systems can lead to significant disruptions if any component fails or malfunctions.
  • Data Privacy Risks: Collecting and analyzing large volumes of data raise concerns about the privacy and protection of personal and proprietary information.
  • Supply Chain Risks: Enhanced interconnectivity and reliance on global supply chains can lead to vulnerabilities, such as disruptions from geopolitical events or natural disasters.
  • Workforce Risks: The shift towards automation may result in job displacement, requiring workforce retraining and addressing the potential social impacts.
